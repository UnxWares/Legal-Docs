## 12. Force majeure

### 12.1. Definition

Neither party may be held liable for a breach of its contractual obligations if such breach results from a case of **force majeure** as defined by Article 1218 of the Civil Code and French case law.

Force majeure cases include:

- Natural disasters (floods, earthquakes, fires, storms)
- Acts of war, riots, attacks, acts of terrorism, insurrections
- General or sector strikes blocking contract execution
- Pandemics and epidemics declared by international or national health authorities
- Major failures of the Internet backbone network, widespread power outage
- Acts or decisions of public authorities, requisitions, embargo
- Major cyberattacks such as massive DDoS, not resulting from negligence of the affected party
- Major failure of a critical supplier (datacenter, network operator)

### 12.2. Obligations in case of force majeure

The party affected by force majeure must:

- Notify the other party in writing as soon as possible (maximum 48 hours)
- Provide all justification and documentation of force majeure
- Make its best efforts to limit its consequences and duration
- Regularly inform the other party of the situation's evolution

The obligations of the parties are suspended during the duration of force majeure, without any compensation being due.

### 12.3. Termination for prolonged force majeure

If force majeure persists beyond **30 calendar days**, each party may terminate the contract by right, without compensation or penalty, by written notification (email or registered letter with acknowledgment of receipt).
